Research Foundation

The evidence-based teaching methods behind this resource

This educational website is built on decades of computing education research from institutions worldwide. Every activity type, from prediction exercises to Parsons problems, is grounded in peer-reviewed studies demonstrating their effectiveness for student learning.

Below we acknowledge the researchers and publications that have shaped how we teach programming and computer science theory.

PRIMM Framework

PRIMM (Predict, Run, Investigate, Modify, Make) is a structured approach to teaching programming that emphasises understanding code before writing it. Students first predict what code will do, then run it to check their understanding, investigate how it works, modify existing code, and finally create their own programs.

Why it helps: Research shows that novice programmers often struggle when asked to write code from scratch too early. PRIMM scaffolds learning by building comprehension skills first, reducing cognitive overload and helping students develop accurate mental models of how programs execute.

Parsons Problems

Parsons Problems present students with jumbled lines of code that must be arranged into the correct order. This approach removes the syntax burden while focusing on program structure and logic.

Why it helps: Studies show Parsons problems are more efficient than code writing for learning, taking less time while achieving equal or better learning outcomes. They reduce extraneous cognitive load (remembering syntax) so students can focus on intrinsic concepts (program logic and structure).

Code Tracing

Code tracing involves manually executing code step-by-step, tracking variable values and program state. This fundamental skill helps students understand how computers actually execute programs.

Why it helps: Research demonstrates a strong hierarchy of programming skills: students must be able to trace code before they can understand it, and understand it before they can write it. Explicit tracing practice builds the mental execution skills that underpin all programming ability.

Worked Examples

Worked examples present complete, step-by-step solutions to problems. Students study these solutions before attempting similar problems themselves.

Why it helps: The "worked example effect" is one of the most robust findings in educational psychology. For novices, studying worked examples is more effective than problem-solving practice because it reduces cognitive load during the critical early stages of learning. As expertise develops, this reverses (the "expertise reversal effect"), which is why our resources progress from examples to independent practice.

Subgoal Labels

Subgoal labels are meaningful labels added to groups of steps in worked examples, helping students recognise the purpose of each section of a solution.

Why it helps: Research shows that adding subgoal labels to worked examples significantly improves learning transfer - students become better at solving new problems they haven't seen before. Labels help students form abstract schemas rather than memorising surface-level steps.

Spaced Repetition

Spaced repetition is a learning technique that reviews information at increasing intervals. Our flashcard revision system uses the SM-2 algorithm to schedule reviews just before you're likely to forget.

Why it helps: The "spacing effect" shows that distributed practice over time leads to dramatically better long-term retention than massed practice (cramming). By targeting weak areas identified from quiz mistakes, students spend their revision time where it matters most.
